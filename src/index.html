About the Medicis

The Medici family first rose to prominence in Florence through their success in commerce and banking in the 13th century. Under Cosimo de' Medici, the family's patronage of art and learning transformed Florence into the cradle of the Renaissance and solidified their political power.

The family produced four popes—Leo X, Clement VII, Pius IV and Leo XI—and intermarried with many of Europe's royal houses. Cosimo's grandson Lorenzo, known as the Magnificent, supported masters such as Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo.

The dynasty's influence reached beyond Italy. Catherine de' Medici became queen of France and three of her sons ruled as kings. The family's later branch produced Cosimo I, who became duke of Florence in 1537 and grand duke of Tuscany in 1569. The last Medici ruler died in 1737, marking the end of nearly three centuries of Medici power.

Notable Members

Cosimo the Elder

Patron of artists and architects; ruled Florence from 1434 until his death.

Lorenzo the Magnificent

Poet and statesman who supported Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci, and Michelangelo.

Catherine de Medici

Queen of France whose sons Henry III, Charles IX and Francis II all became kings.

Legacy

The Medici's support of the arts fostered masterpieces that still define the Renaissance. Cosimo's Uffizi offices became a museum that houses priceless works. Under their patronage, Florence thrived culturally and economically, leaving an enduring legacy.

Even after their political power waned, the family's influence on art, science, and politics echoed across Europe. Today, their palaces, libraries and collections remain central to understanding this transformative era.
